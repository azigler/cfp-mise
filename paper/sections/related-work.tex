\section{Background and Related Work}

Early empirical studies of AI-assisted coding reported substantial productivity
gains: Peng et al.\ found that developers using GitHub Copilot completed tasks
21--55\% faster than a control group~\cite{peng2023copilot}. However, speed has
not translated cleanly into quality. Veracode's 2025 industry analysis found
that 45\% of AI-generated code contains security flaws, with a tenfold spike in
security findings attributed to AI-authored code~\cite{veracode2025ai}.
Research on the human side reveals complementary concerns: Mozannar et al.\
showed that developers' mental models frequently diverge from actual AI
behavior, leading to inappropriate acceptance of incorrect
suggestions~\cite{developer2025mental}, while Vasconcelos et al.\ demonstrated
that trust in AI code generation depends on context-aware explanations that
current tools rarely provide~\cite{facct2024trust}. Together, these findings
point to a productivity paradox: AI agents accelerate output while
simultaneously increasing the surface area for rework and defects. The
bottleneck in agentic coding, we argue, is not code generation but
\emph{alignment}---ensuring that what agents build matches what practitioners
intend.

Several preparation-oriented methodologies have emerged in response.
Horthy's Research-Plan-Implement (RPI) methodology~\cite{horthy2025rpi} most
directly anticipates our work, requiring practitioners to complete discrete
research and planning phases before any code is generated. GitHub's Spec
Kit~\cite{github2025speckit} formalizes specification-driven development, where
a structured specification artifact governs agent behavior throughout
implementation. At a broader level, the discourse has shifted from prompt
engineering---optimizing individual instructions---to \emph{context
engineering}: designing the complete informational environment surrounding an
LLM~\cite{anthropic2025context, gartner2025context}. Alomar et al.\ extend this
trajectory further by proposing \emph{promptware engineering}, the application
of established software engineering principles to the development and
maintenance of prompts as first-class artifacts~\cite{alomar2025promptware}.
Dakhel et al.'s taxonomy of human-AI collaboration identifies eleven distinct
interaction types~\cite{dakhel2025humanai}, providing a classification framework
within which preparation-phase activities can be situated. Collectively, these
contributions recognize that what happens \emph{before} agent execution shapes
outcomes, but none offers a unified, theoretically grounded methodology for the
preparation phase itself.

A parallel stream of practitioner innovation has addressed preparation through
tooling and workflow patterns. Karpathy coined ``vibe
coding''~\cite{karpathy2025vibe} to describe a minimal-preparation workflow in
which the developer ``fully gives in to the vibes'' and trusts the model---an
approach he explicitly scoped to throwaway weekend projects, yet the term
rapidly entered mainstream dictionaries and came to characterize the dominant
mode of AI-assisted development. At the opposite end of the preparation
spectrum, Huntley's Ralph Loop~\cite{huntley2025ralph} provides a pattern of
fine-grained, iterative task delegation to agents, emphasizing persistence over
planning. Yegge's Beads framework~\cite{yegge2025beads} addresses agent memory
through structured task records with dependency tracking, extended by Emanuel's
high-performance Rust reimplementation~\cite{emanuel2025beadsrust}. These tools
solve specific problems---iteration, memory, orchestration---but assume that
appropriate context and specifications already exist.

Our methodology draws on two established theoretical frameworks. Wiggins and
McTighe's \emph{backward design}~\cite{wiggins1998understanding} holds that
effective instruction begins with desired outcomes, then works backward through
assessment criteria to learning activities---a deliberate inversion of the
conventional ``content-first'' approach. We apply this principle directly:
defining agent outputs and success criteria before designing the context that
will produce them. Polanyi's concept of tacit knowledge---``we can know more
than we can tell''~\cite{polanyi1966tacit, polanyi1958personal}---frames the
preparation phase as a knowledge externalization problem. Much of what
experienced developers know about a domain, its constraints, and its value
judgments remains tacit until deliberately articulated into forms that agents
can consume. Agents cannot access knowledge that has not been made explicit;
preparation is the act of making it so.

These four streams---empirical AI-assisted development research, emerging
preparation methodologies, practitioner tooling, and educational
theory---converge on the importance of deliberate preparation but remain
fragmented. No existing work unifies them into a formalized methodology with
theoretical grounding and empirical demonstration. Our mise en place framework
addresses this gap.
