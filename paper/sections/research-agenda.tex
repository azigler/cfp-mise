\section{Research Agenda and Conclusion}

\subsection{Research Agenda}

Mise en place as described here rests on a single case study and conceptual
argument. Empirical validation across practitioners, domains, and agent
models is essential. We propose five research questions:

\begin{description}
  \item[RQ1:] \emph{Does deliberate preparation reduce agent misalignment
    compared to iterative development, and at what cost-benefit threshold?}
    Controlled experiments comparing preparation-first and iteration-first
    workflows could establish whether the upfront investment yields net savings.

  \item[RQ2:] \emph{What constitutes ``sufficient'' context for agentic
    work---can we define stopping criteria for the preparation phase?}
    A saturation-style analysis measuring alignment quality as a function of
    context volume would help practitioners know when to stop preparing.

  \item[RQ3:] \emph{How does context fluency vary across practitioners,
    domains, and agent models?} Comparative studies could reveal whether
    context fluency is trainable or a function of prior domain
    knowledge~\cite{polanyi1958personal}.

  \item[RQ4:] \emph{Can mise en place scale beyond prototyping to sustained,
    multi-month development?} Longitudinal case studies would test the
    methodology's applicability at scale~\cite{dakhel2025humanai}.

  \item[RQ5:] \emph{What is the relationship between preparation time and
    implementation quality across project complexities?} A multi-project
    dataset correlating preparation artifacts with implementation metrics
    could yield quantitative models of the
    preparation-quality relationship~\cite{alomar2025promptware}.
\end{description}

These questions span experimental, observational, and longitudinal designs.
RQ1 is the most immediately tractable, addressable with paired tasks and a
modest participant pool.

\subsection{Conclusion}

Mise en place, as presented here, is a starting framework rather
than a finished methodology---a formalization of practices that
experienced practitioners already converge on
independently~\cite{huntley2025ralph, horthy2025rpi,
yegge2025beads}. The methodology formalizes what Nonaka and
Takeuchi~\cite{nonaka1995knowledge} term externalization---converting
tacit practitioner knowledge into explicit artifacts that agents can
consume. Our case study suggests that deliberate preparation
can compress implementation time and reduce architectural
misalignment, but we claim no generalizability from a single
hackathon. What we do claim is that the alignment problem in agentic
coding is fundamentally a preparation problem, and that the field
would benefit from treating the work before agents write code as a
first-class engineering discipline.

Context fluency offers a lens for understanding emerging practitioner skills---skills
that bridge domain expertise, pedagogical design, and specification
craft~\cite{anthropic2025context, github2025speckit}. We invite empirical
validation, replication across domains, and the development of tools that support
preparation-phase work. The code, increasingly, is the easy part. The hard
part---knowing what to build and why---remains a distinctly human contribution.
