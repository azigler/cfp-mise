\section{Case Study: Competitive Hackathon}
\label{sec:casestudy}

We report on the application of mise en place during a competitive hackathon
to illustrate the methodology's behavior under realistic constraints. The
setting imposed fixed time limits, external evaluation, and a requirement to
produce working software---conditions that favor rapid iteration and
penalize unproductive preparation.

\subsection{Setting}

The hackathon took place in January 2026, organized around a publisher's
editorial archive and an AI-powered content API. Approximately 12 teams
competed in a five-hour window to build working prototypes. A panel of
industry judges evaluated final presentations. The practitioner-researcher
had professional backgrounds in both education and software engineering,
a combination that informed the product concept and design decisions
throughout the event.

\subsection{Preparation Phase}

While most teams began coding within the first fifteen minutes, the
practitioner-researcher spent approximately two hours in deliberate
preparation, applying the three phases described in
Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

\textbf{Contextual grounding} produced 10 planning documents totaling 9,386
words, including API exploration notes, competitive analysis, and---critically---an
extended dictation on pedagogical design philosophy drawn from the practitioner's
teaching experience, encoding tacit knowledge about how effective learning
environments create conditions for student thinking~\cite{polanyi1966tacit}.

\textbf{Collaborative specification} occurred concurrently, as the practitioner
refined the product concept through dialogue with an AI agent. The resulting
specification described a platform where teachers curate bounded research
environments from The Atlantic's archive and students conduct research using a
Socratic AI tutor. Key value judgments encoded in the specification included
constraining the product to a single assignment type (prioritizing depth over
breadth for a three-minute demo),
insisting on real API data rather than mocks, and scoping the analytics dashboard
to show the student's research \emph{journey} rather than only the final submission.

\textbf{Task decomposition} converted the specification into 64 structured
task records with priorities and dependencies. The planning-to-code
ratio---9,386 words of planning against 8,496 lines of source code---was
1.10:1. Table~\ref{tab:timeline} summarizes the timeline.

\subsection{Implementation and Results}

Four parallel subagents were deployed, each responsible for a distinct
feature area: classroom creation, student research workspace,
day-one/\allowbreak day-thirty demo toggle, and Socratic AI tutor. The agents
drew on the contextual grounding documents and specification produced
during preparation.

The 64 task records produced during decomposition served as the direct
interface between preparation and execution---the mechanism by which
Phase~3 artifacts feed parallel agents (Figure~\ref{fig:methodology}).
Each bead encoded an independently executable unit of work derived from
the specification, with boundaries that required no inter-agent
coordination. Bead closure timestamps confirm that this decomposition
enabled genuine parallelism: agents completed work simultaneously across
feature areas, with a median closure time of 5.9~minutes per bead.
The subsequent deployment phase produced 9~commits over 52~minutes.
The final codebase comprised 43 TypeScript/TSX files totaling 8,496
lines, deployed to production as a full-stack educational platform.

Three observations are relevant to the methodology's evaluation. First, the
architecture required no structural refactoring
during the deployment phase. The bugs that emerged were integration and styling
issues---API content truncation, data flow between components, favicon
configuration---not architectural misalignment. This suggests that the
preparation phase successfully transferred the practitioner's architectural
intent to the agents. Second, the preparation-to-execution
time ratio was approximately 5.7:1 (two hours of preparation against approximately
21 minutes of active implementation per agent, across four parallel agents),
indicating a deliberate investment directionally
opposite to the ``start coding immediately'' pattern common in hackathon settings.
Bug-type beads were resolved with a median closure time of 1.2~minutes
(mean 1.5~minutes), compared to 9.7~minutes for implementation tasks,
suggesting that defects introduced during parallel execution were
quickly identified and corrected.
Third, near-zero architectural rework is consistent with the hypothesis that rich
upfront context reduces agent misalignment, though we cannot establish causation
from a single case study.

\subsection{Limitations}

This case study is illustrative rather than definitive. It involves a single
practitioner, a single project, and no control group. We cannot determine
whether the outcome would have differed with less preparation, a different
practitioner, or a different AI model. The hackathon setting, while providing
authentic time pressure, is not representative of sustained multi-month
development. We present these results as suggestive evidence that the
methodology merits further investigation, not as proof of its general efficacy.
Additionally, the practitioner's dual professional background in education and
software engineering likely contributed to the quality of contextual grounding,
particularly the pedagogical domain encoding that informed the Socratic tutor.
Whether mise en place yields equivalent results for practitioners without
specialized domain expertise remains an open question, directly motivating RQ3.
Nevertheless, the methodology's emphasis on deliberate preparation
stands in marked contrast to the iteration-first patterns documented
in practitioner literature~\cite{karpathy2025vibe}---a contrast
that itself motivates formal comparative study.
