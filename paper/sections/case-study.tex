\section{Case Study: Competitive Hackathon}
\label{sec:casestudy}

We report on the application of mise en place during a competitive hackathon
to illustrate the methodology's behavior under realistic constraints. The
setting imposed fixed time limits, external evaluation, and a requirement to
produce working software---conditions that favor rapid iteration and
penalize unproductive preparation.

\subsection{Setting}

The hackathon was co-hosted by The Atlantic, Infactory, and Hacks/Hackers in
January 2026. Approximately 12 teams competed in a five-hour window to build
``trusted news experiences of tomorrow'' using The Atlantic's editorial archive
and Infactory's AI-powered content API. A panel of judges that included
Nicholas Thompson, CEO of The Atlantic, evaluated final presentations. The
practitioner-researcher had professional backgrounds in both education and
software engineering, a combination that informed the product concept and
design decisions throughout the event.

\subsection{Preparation Phase}

While most teams began coding within the first fifteen minutes, the
practitioner-researcher spent approximately two hours in deliberate
preparation, applying the three phases described in
Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

\textbf{Contextual grounding} produced 10 planning documents totaling 9,386
words. These included API exploration notes, UI architecture plans, competitive
analysis of other teams' approaches, domain knowledge capture from a recent
interview with The Atlantic's CEO, and---critically---an extended dictation
on pedagogical design philosophy drawn from the practitioner's teaching
experience. This last document encoded tacit knowledge about how effective
learning environments create conditions for student thinking rather than
delivering answers~\cite{polanyi1966tacit}.

\textbf{Collaborative specification} occurred concurrently with contextual
grounding, as the practitioner refined the product concept through dialogue
with an AI agent. The resulting specification described a platform where
teachers curate bounded research environments from The Atlantic's archive and
students conduct research using a Socratic AI tutor. Key value judgments
encoded in the specification included constraining the product to a single
assignment type (prioritizing depth over breadth for a three-minute demo),
insisting on real API data rather than mocks, and scoping the analytics
dashboard to show the student's research \emph{journey} rather than only
the final submission.

\textbf{Task decomposition} converted the specification into 64 structured
task records (beads) with priorities and dependencies. The planning-to-code
ratio---9,386 words of planning documents against 8,496 lines of source
code---was 1.10:1, indicating that the practitioner produced more planning
content by word count than the agents ultimately produced in code.

\subsection{Implementation Phase}

Four parallel subagents were deployed, each responsible for a distinct feature
area: classroom creation flow, student research workspace, day-one/day-thirty
demo state toggle, and Socratic AI tutor. The agents drew on the contextual
grounding documents and specification produced during preparation.

The first git commit, recorded at 22:24 UTC, contained 76 files and 13,143
lines---the bulk of the codebase produced during the implementation phase
before any code was committed to version control. The subsequent deployment
and polish phase produced 9 commits over 52 minutes, with a mean inter-commit
interval of 6 minutes 32 seconds. The final codebase comprised 43
TypeScript/TSX files totaling 8,496 lines of source code, deployed to a
production environment with real Atlantic archive data served through a
serverless API proxy.

The median bead closure time was 5.9 minutes, indicating that individual
tasks were scoped to a granularity that agents could complete rapidly. Of 68
total beads (64 created during the hackathon plus 4 post-event), 47 were
closed (69.1\%). The 21 remaining open beads were stretch features---secondary
capabilities explicitly deprioritized during task decomposition. All 6 bugs
filed during the event were resolved (100\% bug resolution rate).

\subsection{Results and Observations}

The resulting product, ``The Beat,'' was an educational platform with teacher
dashboards, student research workspaces, article annotation tools, citation
validation, and a Socratic AI tutor. The judges selected it for first place.

Three observations from this case study are relevant to the methodology's
evaluation. First, the architecture was correct on first pass: no structural
refactoring was required during the deployment phase. The bugs that emerged
were integration and styling issues---API content truncation, data flow between
components, favicon configuration---not architectural misalignment. This
suggests that the preparation phase successfully transferred the practitioner's
architectural intent to the agents.

Second, the preparation-to-execution time ratio was approximately 5.7:1
(roughly 17 hours of total session time against 3 hours of agent
implementation and deployment). While this ratio reflects a single
practitioner's workflow and cannot be generalized, it indicates a deliberate
investment in preparation that is directionally opposite to the ``start coding
immediately'' pattern common in hackathon settings.

Third, the near-zero architectural rework is consistent with the hypothesis
that rich upfront context reduces agent misalignment, though we cannot
establish causation from a single uncontrolled case study.

\subsection{Limitations}

This case study is illustrative rather than definitive. It involves a single
practitioner, a single project, and no control group. We cannot determine
whether the outcome would have differed with less preparation, a different
practitioner, or a different AI model. The hackathon setting, while providing
authentic time pressure, is not representative of sustained multi-month
development. We present these results as suggestive evidence that the
methodology merits further investigation, not as proof of its general
efficacy.
