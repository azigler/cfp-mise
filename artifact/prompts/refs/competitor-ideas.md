| Reader Participation as a Trust Engine. **Problem:** Journalists can't be experts in everything they cover, and readers with genuine expertise have no structured way to contribute their knowledge. Comment sections, when they do exist, can be noisy and adversarial (The Atlantic doesn't have one). **Idea:** Build a system that lets readers with relevant firsthand expertise (doctors, engineers, locals, practitioners) flag specific claims they can verify, contest, or add context to. Not a comment section — a structured input where they identify their basis for knowledge and point to specific passages. Moderated and surfaced transparently alongside the article. Turns the audience into an engaged trust asset rather than just passive consumers. **Why this would work:** Readers like participating in the conversation. LLMs make it feasible to surface trusted voices by triaging incoming reader submissions, flagging low-quality or bad-faith contributions, and helping moderators surface signal from noise at scale. | [Team 9] — product / community background | A technical collaborator, and someone with editorial experience to help think through moderation policies. |
| Problem (thinking out loud): Readers often engage with specific arguments made in articles. The Atlantic archive allows navigation article by article, making it hard to follow the argument trail over years. With debates and discussions, nuance is lost and the context collapses. Idea: Build an interaction layer for the Atlantic Archive that will let readers explore how a specific argument has been framed, counter-challenged, and evolved over time. Ideally, it is moving away from search query (matching that argument to other such keywords/topic similarity) to a structured object (understand the passage's role in the context and then search for that kind of idea). Why now? Most discussions originate from a quote or a highlighted phrase within an article. Technological advances help in structured retrieval and language models make it possible to go beyond tokenization and token-based search. This creates an opportunity to expand how readers interact with content. | [Team 8] (second pitch) — product manager | Plans to use Infactory AI. Would like to collaborate with a technical person. |
| **Problem**: Reporters are time-strapped and resource-strapped to produce accurate and in-depth content in a 24-hour news cycle. There is immediate demand for interpretation, at least by engaged audiences. A wealth of knowledge sits in news archives but each story is reported in a moment in time and simple knowledge retrieval doesn't surface insights fast enough. **Solution**: (a) leverage LLMs to produce a knowledge graph based on a news outlet's existing coverage. LLMs can scale processes which would normally require a librarian and time to create/update a knowledge graph. (b) connect this knowledge graph to an AI chatbot that uses another LLM and a graph-search tool. This should create an on-demand "reference copilot" for reporters to leverage context and smart associations via the knowledge graph. | [Team 10] — ex-journalist / current tech product manager | For the Hackathon, is there an API or MCP server we can call to get archived content? |
